Diagonal Triangles (Wedges) are hybrid structures in Elliott Wave Theory that sit between impulses and corrections. They signal shifting market psychology and often mark critical turning points. Recognising diagonals (both leading and ending) helps you spot reversals and high-probability set-ups early ‚Äî essential for any trader studying market psychology and trading geometry.

1Ô∏è‚É£ What Is a Diagonal Triangle?

A Diagonal Triangle (or wedge) is a five-wave structure that can resemble an impulse but breaks some of the standard impulse rules. Key features:

  • Five waves (1‚Äì2‚Äì3‚Äì4‚Äì5)
  • Internal overlap between waves 1 and 4
  • Converging or diverging trendlines (wedge shape)
  • Weaker momentum toward completion

Note: diagonals indicate that a trend is forming under pressure (leading) or that a trend is exhausting (ending). This reflects crowd behaviour in markets and is a powerful signal in elliott wave analysis.

2Ô∏è‚É£ Why Diagonals Matter

Diagonals matter because they appear in two high-impact locations:

  • Leading Diagonal: often in Wave 1 of a new trend or Wave A of a correction ‚Äî suggests a fragile start to a new phase.
  • Ending Diagonal: in Wave 5 of an impulse or Wave C ‚Äî signals exhaustion and a likely sharp reversal.

3Ô∏è‚É£ Internal Structure

Diagonals usually subdivide as 3-3-3-3-3 (each internal leg has three waves). Unlike impulses (5-3-5-3-5), diagonals look choppier and allow overlap ‚Äî that overlap is the defining visual clue.

5Ô∏è‚É£ Termination ‚Äî Ending Diagonal

An Ending Diagonal forms in Wave 5 or Wave C. It shows exhaustion: price moves in the trend direction but loses conviction. Characteristics include subdivision into threes, overlap of Wave 1 & 4, decreasing volume, and often a violent reversal after breakout.

Psychology & Fibonacci Observations

Ending diagonals often appear at emotional extremes (blow-off tops or panic lows). Fibonacci relationships can hold: Wave 5 may equal Wave 3 or be ~0.618 of Waves 1‚Äì3 combined.

6Ô∏è‚É£ Leading Diagonal (Type 1 & Type 2)

A Leading Diagonal appears at a fresh start (Wave 1 or Wave A). There are two variations:

  • Type 1 (Corrective internal ‚Äî 3-3-3-3-3): fragile start, often followed by a deep Wave 2 retracement.
  • Type 2 (Impulsive internal ‚Äî 5-3-5-3-5): stronger-looking; more decisive reversal backed by volume.

7Ô∏è‚É£ Rules for Diagonal Triangles

Diagonals still obey core Elliott rules (with diagonal-specific constraints):

  • Rule 1: Wave 4 always overlaps Wave 1 (diagonals permit overlap).
  • Rule 2: Wave 3 cannot be the shortest among 1, 3, 5.
  • Rule 3: Converging or diverging trendlines must be present (draw 1‚Üí3 and 2‚Üí4 to verify).
  • Rule 4: Wave 5 often pokes beyond the wedge before the reversal occurs.

8Ô∏è‚É£ Identifying Diagonals in Real Charts

Checklist when suspecting a diagonal:

  • Five distinct waves present
  • Wave 4 overlaps Wave 1
  • Each leg subdivides into three waves (or shows mixed internal structure)
  • Trendlines converge/diverge
  • Momentum (RSI/MACD) weakens into final wave
  • Volume dries up toward completion

9Ô∏è‚É£ Trading Insights

Diagonals are not guaranteed trade signals but provide strong context:

  • Ending Diagonals: look for RSI/MACD divergence and a wedge trendline break ‚Äî target a retrace to the diagonal origin.
  • Leading Diagonals: wait for Wave 2 correction to enter; common entry near 61.8‚Äì78.6% retracement of the diagonal range.
  • Stops: below the start of Wave 1 (for long entries on leading diagonals).
  • Targets: common target methods include equality (Wave 5 ‚âà Wave 3) or 1.618 √ó diagonal height projected from Wave 2.

üîë Chapter Summary

Diagonal Triangles bridge impulses and corrections. They come in two flavours ‚Äî leading (start of trends) and ending (trend exhaustion). Their internal 3-3-3-3-3 subdivision and the overlap of waves 1 & 4 are the key identification clues. Use momentum divergence and proper trendline breaks for trade confirmation. Mastering diagonals improves your Elliott wave analysis and understanding of crowd behaviour in markets.
